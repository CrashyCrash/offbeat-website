1. Audio-Technica ATH-M20x — Best Overall DJ Headphone Under $50

The ATH-M20x is the entry point of Audio-Technica's M-series — the same line that produced the studio-standard M50x. The closed-back design provides enough passive noise isolation to hear your cue in a moderately loud environment, and the frequency response is tuned for music monitoring rather than consumer-style bass boosting.

The 40mm drivers produce clear highs and defined midrange, which matters when you're listening for pitch and beatmatch while the PA is running. The low end is present but controlled — you'll hear kicks and basslines without the exaggerated thump that makes mix errors harder to detect.

The main trade-off: no rotating ear cups means you can't do proper single-ear monitoring by flipping one cup behind your ear. DJs who shoulder-monitor will need to work around this. The 3m cable is also slightly long for desktop practice but works fine when standing at a controller.

Pros

  • Genuine studio pedigree — same driver series as the M50x
  • Closed-back design with real noise isolation
  • Flat-ish response that rewards careful listening
  • Extremely durable for the price

Cons

  • No cup rotation or swivel — single-ear monitoring is awkward
  • 3m cable length is overkill for practice setups
  • Ear pads wear out in 12-18 months with heavy use

Best for: Beginning DJs who want their first serious pair without breaking the bank. They'll grow with you for 2-3 years before you'll feel the limitations.

2. OneOdio Pro-10 — Best for Scratch DJs and Portability

The OneOdio Pro-10 is the hidden gem of this list. At $33, it includes both a coiled cable AND a straight cable — a dual-cable setup you typically see on headphones costing three times as much. The coiled cable coils from the left ear cup and is the right length for working close to a mixer; the straight cable is better for portable listening.

The 50mm drivers produce pronounced bass — more than the ATH-M20x. For mixing bass-heavy genres (techno, hip-hop, reggaeton), this works in your favor: kicks sound fat and bassline changes are obvious. For more dynamic genres, the extra low-end can occasionally mask high-frequency detail.

The ear cups rotate 90° for single-ear monitoring, which is the standard technique for beatmatching on club gear. This alone makes the Pro-10 the better pick for DJs who've already developed basic mixing instincts and want to start using the cue system properly.

Pros

  • Rotating ear cups — full single-ear monitoring capability
  • Both coiled and straight cables included
  • Large 50mm drivers with strong bass response
  • Folds flat for transport

Cons

  • Bass-heavy tuning can exaggerate mix errors in treble-heavy tracks
  • Slightly bulkier than competitors at the same price
  • Build quality improvement opportunity: plastic hinge feels marginal

Best for: Scratch DJs, DJs who use the one-ear monitor technique, and DJs who carry their headphones to gigs or open deck nights.

3. Sennheiser HD 206 — Best for Ear Training

The HD 206 is different from the others on this list: it's not tuned for monitoring dance music, it's tuned for accuracy. The flat frequency response means bass doesn't get exaggerated and high hats sound like high hats. For DJs actively working on their ear — learning to count bars, identify key signatures, or hear pitch variations — the honest sound of the HD 206 is a genuine training advantage.

At 145g, it's also the lightest headphone on this list, which matters during two-hour practice sessions. The lightweight build does sacrifice some durability compared to the ATH-M20x — the headband is thinner and the ear pads compress faster — but for bedroom practice, this is a reasonable trade-off.

Pros

  • Flat response — trains your ear accurately
  • Lightest pair on this list at 145g
  • Great value for ear training and sound awareness

Cons

  • No rotating cups — limited for live DJ technique
  • Less durable than ATH-M20x

4. AKG K52 — Honest Studio Tuning on a Shoestring

AKG (now owned by Samsung subsidiary Harman) produces headphones for both studio and consumer markets. The K52 sits at the very bottom of their studio catalog, but retains the AKG house sound: detailed mids, present but not dominant bass, and surprisingly clear stereo imaging for a $29 headphone.

For DJs who want to cross-train their ear between DJing and music production, the K52 gives accurate enough monitoring to use in both contexts. The large ear cups fully enclose the ear, which helps with isolation in noisier environments.

Best for: DJ-producers who do both roles and want one headphone that covers both contexts without sounding like a consumer product.

5. Sony MDR-7506 — The Wild Card ($49 on Sale)

The Sony MDR-7506 is a professional broadcast and studio monitor that normally retails at $79-$89. It periodically drops to $49 during Amazon sales and holiday weeks. If you're reading this within range of a sale price, it's the clear best option under $50 — better than everything else on this list at its regular price point.

The MDR-7506 folds, has rotating cups for single-ear monitoring, uses a coiled cable, and has a 30-year track record in broadcast studios. The K+M 40mm drivers are tuned for critical listening, not fun consumer listening. Set up a price alert and buy it the moment it drops to $49.

Best for: Anyone willing to wait 2-4 weeks for a sale price — this is the best headphone under $99 that occasionally comes under $50.

Who These Headphones Are For

Headphones under $50 are the right choice if:

  • You're in the first 6 months of learning to DJ and don't know what gear you'll actually use
  • You need a practice pair for bedroom sessions and already own better headphones for gigs
  • You're buying for a young person learning DJ basics and don't want to risk expensive equipment
  • You host open decks at your bar/venue and need "house headphones" that can take abuse

Skip the sub-$50 category if: you're playing gigs, you mix in loud environments, or you already have 6+ months of experience. The step up to the $100-150 range (Pioneer HDJ-X5, Sennheiser HD 25) gives substantial performance gains worth the extra investment.

What to Look For in Budget DJ Headphones

Closed-Back vs. Open-Back

For DJing, always choose closed-back headphones. Closed-back designs seal the ear cup against ambient sound, which means the music playing in the headphones doesn't leak into the room (matters when cueing tracks with a microphone nearby) and venue noise doesn't overwhelm your cue channel. Open-back headphones are designed for studio mixing in quiet environments — not for monitoring while a PA system is running.

Driver Size

Larger drivers (40-50mm) generally produce more bass, which is relevant for dance music monitoring. However, driver size alone doesn't determine sound quality — the tuning and the materials matter more. A well-tuned 36mm driver (like the Sennheiser HD 206) outperforms a poorly tuned 50mm driver for accuracy.

Single-Ear Monitoring Capability

In professional DJ technique, you pre-listen to the next track in one ear while hearing the room through the other ear. This "shoulder mounting" or "one-ear cup behind the ear" technique requires either rotating ear cups (preferred) or headphones with enough flexibility in the headband to fold one cup back. None of the sub-$50 headphones are perfect for this, but the OneOdio Pro-10's rotating cups are the best option at the price.

Cable Type

For DJ practice: coiled cables are traditionally preferred because they retract when not in use and extend to reach a controller or CDJ without catching on gear. For general listening: straight cables are more convenient. If possible, choose headphones that include both (like the OneOdio Pro-10) or come with a replaceable cable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need special DJ headphones to learn?

No. Any closed-back headphones with decent isolation work for learning. You mainly need to hear the cue channel clearly without the master channel bleeding in. The headphones on this list are all adequate for 6-12 months of learning — you'll naturally outgrow them as your technique develops.

What's the difference between DJ headphones and studio headphones?

DJ headphones typically have bass-boosted tuning, single-ear swivel functionality, rotating ear cups, and coiled cables. Studio headphones aim for flat, accurate frequency response. Either can work for learning to DJ — DJ-tuned headphones feel more natural for dance music monitoring; studio headphones train your ear more accurately for pitch and mix quality.

Can I use Bluetooth headphones for DJing?

No. Bluetooth headphones introduce 20-200ms of audio latency, which makes cue monitoring impossible — you'll hear the track starting 100+ milliseconds after the beats you're trying to align. All DJ headphones in this guide are wired, which is a hard requirement for the cue channel in any real DJ setup.

Are $50 headphones worth buying or should I save for better ones?

Worth buying if you're a complete beginner. The ATH-M20x at $49 is a genuine quality product that won't hold you back for your first year of learning. The step up to Pioneer HDJ-X5 ($119) or Sennheiser HD 25 ($199) is meaningful but not necessary before you have consistent practice habits. Spend $49 now, learn for a year, then upgrade with informed experience about what matters to you.

What happens if my headphones break at a gig?

Carry a backup pair in your kit bag. At the $30-50 price point, keeping two pairs on hand costs less than a single mid-range pair. The OneOdio Pro-10 makes a good backup headphone — it's cheap enough to buy two, small enough to fold flat, and includes replacement cables.

Are these headphones good for music production too?

The Sennheiser HD 206 and AKG K52 are reasonable for basic production work due to their flat response. The ATH-M20x is acceptable but not ideal. The OneOdio Pro-10's bass boost makes it less suitable for production mixing decisions. For production, the next step up is the Sony MDR-7506 or the ATH-M50x — the latter now has a more accurate version (M50x Monitor).
